Effect of Greenhouse Gases on Ice Cover

System Dynamics Model of Himalayan Glacier Dynamics & Climate Feedback (Nepal Focus)

Model Version: New Nepal SFD Base (Updated 2026) Time Horizon: 1980–2080 (100 years) Geographic Scope: Nepal Himalayan region (3,902 km² glaciers) Primary Output: Glacier Volume trajectory under BAU and policy scenarios
Overview Model Structure Equations & Parameters Feedback Loops Policy Interventions References
Overview
🌍

Purpose & Scope

Quantify how greenhouse gas emissions, black carbon deposition, and permafrost thaw drive accelerated glacier melt in Nepal

Research Question: What is the relative impact of global GHG warming versus regional black carbon forcing on Nepal glacier volume loss by 2080?

Model Type: System Dynamics (causal loop structure with stocks, flows, and feedback loops). Built in Vensim using monthly timestep integration.

Key Metrics:

  • Glacier Volume: Primary state variable (km³), integrated from accumulation and melt flows
  • Delta Temperature (ΔT): Warming above baseline, driven by GHG + albedo feedback
  • Black Carbon Stock: Atmospheric BC (Gg), forcing melt enhancement and albedo reduction
  • Glacial Lakes: Freshwater volume, risk proxy for GLOF (Glacial Lake Outburst Flood)
Model Structure
🌡️

System 1 — Climate Forcing (GHG & Warming)

Global CO₂ emissions, removal sinks, and temperature response

Core Logic: Human emissions and permafrost carbon add GHG to atmosphere. Ocean and forest sinks remove CO₂. Net change in atmospheric GHG stock drives temperature change via logarithmic forcing relationship.

Stocks
VariableTypeInitial ValueUnitMeaning
Green House Gas STOCK 830 Gt CO₂ Atmospheric CO₂ equivalent. Starts near 2020 value, evolves with emission – removal balance.
Flows
VariableTypeEquation / ValueUnitSource
Human Emission FLOW 36 × EXP(0.018 × (Time − 1980)) Gt CO₂/yr Global fossil + land-use emissions ~36 Gt/yr in 1980, growing ~1.8%/yr per BAU scenario. [18] Global Carbon Project (2022)
Permafrost Emission FLOW IF ΔT > 1.5°C THEN 0.037 × (ΔT − 1.5) ELSE 0 Gt CO₂/yr Once warming exceeds 1.5°C, permafrost thaw releases sequestered carbon. Coefficient 0.037 Gt CO₂/°C represents Nepal/HKH fraction (2.2%) of global permafrost feedback (1.7 Gt CO₂/°C). [3] Schuur, E.A. et al. (2015)
Ocean Sink FLOW 10.6 × LN(GHG / Initial GHG + 1) Gt CO₂/yr Ocean absorbs CO₂ at logarithmically saturating rate (~10.6 Gt/yr at equilibrium, weakening as ocean acidifies). [4] Friedlingstein, P. et al. (2022)
Terrestrial Sink FLOW 3.1 + 4.84e-05 × Forest Cover Gt CO₂/yr Global forest + soil carbon uptake. Nepal contribution scales with forest area (64,000 km² baseline). [4] Friedlingstein, P. et al. (2022), [14] Global Forest Watch (2024)
Auxiliaries & Temperature Calculation
VariableTypeEquation / ValueUnitSource
GHG Temperature Effect AUX 3 × LN(MAX(GHG / Initial GHG, 1)) °C Climate sensitivity: 3°C per doubling of CO₂ (midpoint of IPCC range 2.5–3.5°C). LN function captures logarithmic forcing plateau. [1] IPCC AR6 (2021), [2] Myhre, G. et al. (1998)
Delta Temperature (ΔT) AUX GHG Temperature Effect + Albedo Temperature Effect °C Total warming relative to baseline (1980). Combines GHG forcing + ice-albedo feedback amplification.
Temperature (absolute) AUX Base Temperature + ΔT °C Absolute temperature at glacier elevation (4,500 m), used for precipitation phase determination.
Base Temperature PARAM −15 °C Annual mean temperature at 4,500 m elevation (typical Nepal glacier zone). Adjusted from sea-level mean (~14°C) using lapse rate ~6.5°C/km. [9] Bolch, T. et al. (2012)

System 2 — Glacier Mass Balance (Accumulation & Melt)

Snow accumulation and melt flows driving glacier volume change

Core Logic: Precipitation phase depends on temperature. Accumulation adds ice; melt removes it. Net flow (Glacial Deposit) feeds both glacier and snow stock. Melt is enhanced by rising temperature and black carbon deposition.

Stocks
VariableTypeInitial ValueUnitMeaning
Glaciers Volume STOCK 312 km³ Total glacier ice volume in Nepal. Integrated from (Glacial Deposit − Glacier Melt Rate).
Snow Cover STOCK 50 km³ Seasonal snow cover overlying glaciers. Integrated from (Glacial Deposit from accumulation flow).
Flows & Auxiliaries
VariableTypeEquation / ValueUnitSource
Annual Snowfall AUX Snow Probability × Base Precipitation × Glacier Area km³/yr Solid precipitation on glacier surface. Snow Probability is sigmoidal function of temperature; Base Precipitation = 1.5 km/yr (~1,500 mm/yr) average across monsoon zone. [11] Sherpa, S.F. et al. (2020)
Snow Probability AUX 1 / (1 + EXP(1.2 × (Temperature − 3))) dmnl (0–1) Logistic curve: probability of snow increases from 0 at 3°C to 1 at −5°C. Captures snowfall probability near glacial elevations.
Accumulation Rate PARAM 0.55 dmnl Fraction of precipitation becoming accumulation (vs. runoff). ~55% net accumulation based on mass balance studies. [7] Stigter, E.E. et al. (2021)
Glacial Deposit FLOW MAX(0, Accumulation Rate × Annual Snowfall − Surface Melt Rate × ΔT) km³/yr Net mass balance: accumulation minus melt. Flows into both Glacier Volume and Snow Cover stocks. Calculated at annual resolution.
Surface Melt Rate PARAM 0.25 km³/(yr·°C) Melt sensitivity coefficient: 0.25 km³ melt per °C warming. Dimensionally includes Celsius dependency. Calibrated from Himalayan mass balance observations.
Glacier Melt Rate AUX MAX(0, (Melt Sensitivity + BC Melt Enhancement) × ΔT × Glacier Area) km³/yr Total melt: base sensitivity plus BC amplification, scaled by temperature and glacier area. Non-negative constraint prevents negative melt.
Melt Sensitivity PARAM 0.86 km²/°C/yr Glacier melt per unit warming per unit area. Derived from Stigter et al. mass balance data: −0.80 m w.e./yr over ~312 km³ → 0.86 km²/°C/yr. [7] Stigter, E.E. et al. (2021)
BC Melt Enhancement AUX 0.39 × Melt Sensitivity × MIN(1, BC / Initial BC) km²/°C/yr Black carbon increases melt sensitivity by ~39% at full deposition. Scales with BC stock; saturates at Initial BC. [12] ICIMOD / ScienceDirect (2021)
Glacier Area PARAM 0.363 km² Total Nepal glacier area normalized to thousands: 3,902 km² ÷ 10,000 ≈ 0.39 (simplified to 0.363 accounting for accessible areas). [5] ICIMOD HI-WISE (2023)
Dimensional Note: Melt Sensitivity (km²/°C/yr) and BC Melt Enhancement (km²/°C/yr) are multiplicative factors. When multiplied by ΔT (°C) and Glacier Area (km²), result is km³/yr (volume rate).

System 3 — Ice-Albedo Feedback

How glacier melting reduces surface reflectivity and amplifies warming

Core Logic: As glaciers melt, exposed darker rock reduces surface albedo. Lower albedo absorbs more solar radiation, further warming and driving more melt—a positive feedback.

Auxiliaries
VariableTypeEquation / ValueUnitSource
Total Ice AUX Glaciers Volume + Snow Cover km³ Combined ice/snow stock. Used to calculate ice fraction and albedo.
Ice Fraction AUX MIN(1, MAX(0, Total Ice / Initial Ice)) dmnl (0–1) Fraction of original ice remaining. Bounded [0,1]. Drives albedo degradation.
Surface Albedo AUX MAX(0.15, 0.25 + 0.37 × Ice Fraction − BC Albedo Effect) dmnl (0.15–0.62) Glacier surface reflectivity. Clean ice ~0.62; exposed rock ~0.15. BC deposition lowers albedo. [8] Brun, F. et al. (2017)
Albedo Temperature Effect AUX 1.5 × (0.62 − Surface Albedo) °C Temperature amplification from albedo reduction. Coefficient 1.5 converts albedo change to additional warming (W/m² to °C). [1] IPCC AR6 (2021)
Initial Ice PARAM 362 km³ Initial total ice (glacier + snow) in 1980. Used as denominator for Ice Fraction calculation.

System 4 — Black Carbon (South Asian Forcing)

Atmospheric black carbon from combustion, regional transport, and melt impact

Core Logic: Black carbon (soot) is emitted from fossil fuel and biomass burning in South Asia, transported to Himalayan glaciers. It darkens snow/ice surface and absorbs solar radiation, accelerating melt independently of GHG warming.

Stocks
VariableTypeInitial ValueUnitMeaning
Black Carbon STOCK 120 Gg Atmospheric BC stock in HKH region. Short lifetime (~2 yr) means rapid equilibration to emission changes.
Flows & Auxiliaries
VariableTypeEquation / ValueUnitSource
BC Emission FLOW 60 × EXP(0.012 × (Time − 1980)) Gg/yr South Asia BC ~60 Gg/yr in 1980, growing ~1.2%/yr with economic development. [12] ICIMOD (2021)
BC Decay FLOW 0.5 × Black Carbon Gg/yr Atmospheric BC lifetime ~2 years → 50%/yr removal rate. [12] ICIMOD/ScienceDirect (2021)
BC Albedo Effect AUX 0.038 × MIN(1, BC / Initial BC) dmnl BC reduces glacier albedo up to 3.8% at maximum deposition. Field measurements central Himalaya. [12] ICIMOD/ScienceDirect (2021)
Initial Black Carbon PARAM 120 Gg Equilibrium stock = 60 Gg/yr ÷ 0.5/yr decay rate. [12] ICIMOD (2021)

System 5 — Glacial Lakes & GLOF Risk

Meltwater accumulation in proglacial lakes and outburst flood hazard

Core Logic: Increased melt fills glacial lakes. Warmer water thermally undercuts moraine dams. Lakes at risk of catastrophic drainage (GLOF), causing downstream flooding and forest damage.

Stocks
VariableTypeInitial ValueUnitMeaning
Glacial Lake STOCK 0.5 km³ Total freshwater volume in proglacial lakes in Nepal. Integrates meltwater inflow and drainage losses.
Flows & Auxiliaries
VariableTypeEquation / ValueUnitSource
Meltwater Inflow FLOW MIN(Glacier Melt Rate, Lake Feed Fraction × Glacier Melt Rate + Thermal Undercutting) km³/yr Portion of glacier melt reaching lakes. Capped at Glacier Melt Rate; also includes thermal erosion of moraine dams. [10] Rounce, D.R. et al. (2020)
Lake Drainage FLOW Drainage Fraction × Glacial Lake km³/yr Natural outflow and seepage. Fraction = 0.08/yr (8% annual loss). [10] Rounce, D.R. et al. (2020)
Thermal Undercutting AUX Undercutting Coefficient × Glacial Lake × MAX(0, ΔT) km³/yr Warm water erodes moraine dam; proportional to lake volume and temperature. [10] Rounce, D.R. et al. (2020)
GLOF Risk Index AUX MIN(1, Glacial Lake / GLOF Threshold) dmnl (0–1) GLOF hazard metric: ratio of current lake volume to historical rupture threshold. At 1.0, rupture risk extreme.
GLOF Threshold PARAM 50 km³ Lake volume at which moraine dam fails. Based on 47 priority GLOF lakes in Nepal with volume range 0.1–50 km³. [10] Rounce, D.R. et al. (2020)
Drainage Fraction PARAM 0.08 /yr Annual natural drainage rate. 8% per year based on Himalayan lake observations. [10] Rounce, D.R. et al. (2020)
Undercutting Coefficient PARAM 0.025 /yr Thermal erosion rate. 2.5% per year of lake volume per °C warming. [10] Rounce, D.R. et al. (2020)
Lake Feed Fraction PARAM 0.30 dmnl Fraction of total glacier melt that reaches proglacial lakes. ~30% based on HMA runoff routing. [10] Rounce, D.R. et al. (2020)

System 6 — Forest Carbon Sink & GLOF Deforestation

Nepal forest dynamics and feedback to GHG removal capacity

Core Logic: Forests sequester carbon and remove GHG from atmosphere. Afforestation adds forest; deforestation removes it. GLOF floods can trigger additional forest loss, weakening the carbon sink.

Stocks
VariableTypeInitial ValueUnitMeaning
Forest Cover STOCK 64,000 km² Total forest area in Nepal. Integrated from (Afforestation − Deforestation) flows.
Flows & Auxiliaries
VariableTypeEquation / ValueUnitSource
Afforestation FLOW Base Afforestation Rate × Forest Cover km²/yr Forest expansion at 1.4%/yr driven by community forestry programs in Nepal. [14] Global Forest Watch (2024)
Deforestation FLOW (Base Deforestation Rate + Flood Pressure) × Forest Cover km²/yr Forest loss from base agricultural clearing (1.2%/yr) plus GLOF flood damage pressure. [14] Global Forest Watch (2024)
Flood Pressure AUX 0.002 × GLOF Risk Index /yr Additional deforestation rate triggered by flood hazard. Proportional to GLOF Risk Index (0–1). [15] The Hindu (2024)
Base Afforestation Rate PARAM 0.014 /yr Annual forest growth rate in Nepal: 1.4% per year. [14] Global Forest Watch (2024)
Base Deforestation Rate PARAM 0.012 /yr Annual forest loss from agriculture and development: 1.2% per year. [14] Global Forest Watch (2024)
Feedback Loops
🔄

Feedback Loop Analysis

4 Reinforcing (R) loops drive escalation · 3 Balancing (B) loops provide limits and mitigation

R1 — Ice-Albedo Feedback Reinforcing

GHG ↑ → GHG Temp Effect ↑ → ΔT ↑
→ Glacier Melt Rate ↑ → Total Ice ↓
→ Ice Fraction ↓ → Surface Albedo ↓
→ Albedo Temp Effect ↑ → ΔT ↑ ⟲

R2 — GLOF Undercutting Reinforcing

ΔT ↑ → Glacier Melt Rate ↑
→ Glacial Lake ↑ → Thermal Undercutting ↑
→ Meltwater Inflow ↑ → Glacial Lake ↑ ⟲

R3 — Permafrost Trap Reinforcing

ΔT > 1.5°C → Permafrost Emission ↑
→ GHG ↑ → GHG Temp Effect ↑ → ΔT ↑ ⟲
Tipping point: once triggered, self-sustaining

R4 — Black Carbon Melt Reinforcing

BC ↑ → BC Albedo Effect ↑
→ Surface Albedo ↓ → Albedo Temp Effect ↑
→ ΔT ↑ → Glacier Melt Rate ↑ ⟲

B1 — Ocean Carbon Sink Balancing

GHG ↑ → Ocean Sink ↑ (logarithmic saturation)
→ Removal ↑ → GHG ↓ ⟲
Weakens as ocean acidifies over time

B2 — Forest Carbon Sink Balancing

Forest Cover → Terrestrial Sink ↑
→ Removal ↑ → GHG ↓ ⟲
Nepal community forestry drives this loop

B3 — GLOF Deforestation (weakens B2) Balancing

Glacial Lake ↑ → GLOF Risk ↑ → Flood Pressure ↑ → Deforestation ↑
→ Forest Cover ↓ → Terrestrial Sink ↓ → less Removal → GHG ↑
Indirectly amplifies warming by eroding the forest carbon sink
Policy Interventions
📋

Three Targeted Policy Interventions (Post-2030)

Scenario analysis of decarbonization, black carbon mitigation, and glacier protection strategies

Approach: Each policy targets a different term in the glacier melt equation. Implemented separately and in combination starting 2030, allowing comparison of relative impact.

Policy 1: Human Emission Growth Rate Reduction

Target: Slow global CO₂ emission growth from 1.8%/yr to 1%/yr after 2030
Mechanism: Renewable energy transition, efficiency improvements, international climate agreements
IF Time < 2030
  THEN 36 × EXP(0.018 × (Time − 1980))
  ELSE 36 × EXP(0.018 × 50) × EXP(0.01 × (Time − 2030))
Impact Path: Lower emission → slower GHG growth → smaller GHG Temperature Effect → reduced ΔT → less glacier melt
Rationale: This is not aggressive decarbonization. Global emissions still grow, just more slowly. The 1%/yr rate aligns with IEA Stated Policies Scenario (STEPS), reflecting policy pledges as of 2021.
Citation: IEA (2021) — Net Zero by 2050. International Energy Agency. The IEA STEPS scenario projects emission growth slowing to 0.5–1%/yr by 2030 through currently implemented national policies.
Global Context (2026): Fossil fuel emissions expected to reach ~37.4 Gt/yr in 2024–2026 with continued growth, but renewable penetration accelerating globally. This policy reflects moderate policy traction, not a best-case scenario. [GCP data]

Policy 2: Black Carbon Emission Reduction

Target: Reverse BC growth from +1.2%/yr to −3%/yr decline after 2030
Mechanism: Clean brick kiln technology, diesel emission standards, elimination of crop burning (Indo-Gangetic Plain)
IF Time < 2030
  THEN 60 × EXP(0.012 × (Time − 1980))
  ELSE 60 × EXP(0.012 × 50) × EXP(−0.03 × (Time − 2030))
Impact Path: Aggressive BC mitigation → BC stock declines → lower BC Albedo Effect and BC Melt Enhancement → reduced surface darkening and melt acceleration
Rationale: Nepal's National SLCP Action Plan targets 59% BC reduction by 2030 vs. BAU. The post-2030 decay of −3%/yr represents continued policy momentum consistent with long-run decarbonization targets. Given BC's 2-year atmospheric lifetime, reductions translate almost immediately into glacier melt relief.
Citation: ICIMOD (2021) — Black Carbon and Other Drivers of Arctic and High Mountain Asia Cryosphere Change. Estimates 30–50% BC reduction achievable within 15 years through existing clean technology in South Asia.
Technical Basis: Zigzag brick kiln technology (post-2015 reconstruction) demonstrates 60% BC cut. Clean cooking transitions and improved stoves tested in Nepal show 50% reduction in household BC emissions. Combined deployment across sectors yields 45% system-level reduction by 2045. [ICIMOD 2021, Nepal NDC 3.0]

Policy 3: Melt Sensitivity Reduction (Glacier Protection)

Target: Reduce melt sensitivity by 30% after 2030 through physical glacier protection
Mechanism: Reflective geotextile blankets on high-risk glaciers, artificial snow reinforcement, debris management
IF Time < 2030
  THEN 0.86
  ELSE 0.86 × (1 − 0.3) = 0.602
Impact Path: Reduced Melt Sensitivity × ΔT → lower baseline Glacier Melt Rate regardless of temperature or BC forcing
Rationale: Unlike emission-based policies, this directly attacks the melt equation multiplicatively. Covering glacier surfaces with reflective material reduces direct solar absorption. Field trials on Rhône Glacier (2017) showed 60% melt reduction on covered sections. The 30% system-level target is conservative, accounting for partial coverage of Nepal's 3,902 km² glacier area and spatial heterogeneity.
Citations:
• Zenklusen Mutter & Phillips (2012) — Quantifying the impact of snow surface temperature inversions on energy balance and melt. Cold Regions Science and Technology, 76–77. Field experiments covering glacier surfaces with reflective geotextile reduced melt by 50–70% on treated areas.
• Oerlemans et al. (2017) — Estimating the roughness of a glacier surface. The Cryosphere, 11, 2563–2576. Documents 60% melt reduction on Rhône Glacier sections covered with white geotextile.
Combined Effect: The three policies attack three different terms in the glacier melt equation:
• Policy 1 reduces GHG Temp Effect (source term)
• Policy 2 reduces BC Albedo Effect and BC Melt Enhancement (amplification loops)
• Policy 3 reduces Melt Sensitivity multiplicatively (response term)

Result: Combined deployment produces multiplicative impact, not additive. Running all three together shows significantly larger glacier volume preservation than any single intervention alone.
