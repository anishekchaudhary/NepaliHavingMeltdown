Nepal Himalayan Glacier System Dynamics Model

CS752 System Dynamics  |  IIT Bombay  |  2024–25

Period: 1980–2080 TIME STEP: 0.125 Year 6 Stocks 4 Reinforcing Loops 3 Balancing Loops

1 Model Overview

This model simulates the cascade of effects from greenhouse gas emissions on Nepal's Himalayan glaciers over the period 1980–2080. It integrates six interconnected subsystems: GHG accumulation, temperature and albedo dynamics, glacier and snow mass balance, glacial lake expansion and GLOF risk, forest carbon sink, and black carbon deposition. The model is implemented in Vensim and uses a TIME STEP of 0.125 Year (Euler integration) with all stocks initialized to 1980 conditions from field data and IPCC/ICIMOD assessments.


The model captures four self-reinforcing feedback loops that accelerate glacier loss — ice-albedo amplification (R1), glacial lake thermal undercutting (R2), permafrost carbon release (R3), and black carbon albedo reduction (R4) — moderated by two balancing sinks (ocean B1 and terrestrial B2) and one indirect balancing mechanism through GLOF-driven deforestation (B3) that weakens the forest sink. The primary purpose is to explore how different emission reduction and land management policies affect Nepal's glacier trajectory through 2080.

2 Feedback Loops

R1 — Ice-Albedo Feedback

Reinforcing
GHG ↑ → Temperature ↑ → Glacier Melt ↑
→ Total Ice ↓ → Surface Albedo ↓
→ Albedo Temp Effect ↑ → Temperature ↑

Source: Bolch et al. (2012), IPCC AR6

R2 — GLOF Undercutting Loop

Reinforcing
Temperature ↑ → Glacier Melt ↑
→ Glacial Lake ↑ → Thermal Undercutting ↑
→ Meltwater Inflow ↑ → Glacial Lake ↑

Source: Rounce et al. PNAS (2020)

R3 — Permafrost Trap

Reinforcing
ΔT > 1.5°C → Permafrost Emission ↑
→ GHG ↑ → Temperature ↑ → ΔT ↑

Source: Schuur et al. (2015)

R4 — Black Carbon Melt Enhancement

Reinforcing
BC Emission ↑ → Black Carbon ↑
→ BC Albedo Effect ↑ → Surface Albedo ↓
→ Temperature ↑ → Glacier Melt ↑

Source: ICIMOD (2021)

B1 — Ocean Carbon Sink

Balancing
GHG ↑ → Ocean Sink ↑ (log saturation)
→ Removal ↑ → GHG ↓

Source: Friedlingstein et al. (2022)

B2 — Forest Carbon Sink

Balancing
Forest Cover → Terrestrial Sink ↑
→ Removal ↑ → GHG ↓

Source: GFW (2024), Friedlingstein (2022)

B3 — GLOF-Deforestation (Weakens B2)

Balancing / Undermining
Glacial Lake ↑ → GLOF Risk ↑ → Flood Pressure ↑ → Deforestation ↑ → Forest Cover ↓ → Terrestrial Sink ↓

Source: ICIMOD HI-WISE (2023)

3 Subsystem Variable Reference

System 1

GHG Accumulation

Tracks the global atmospheric CO2 stock. Nepal's glaciers respond to global warming driven by accumulated GHGs from all sources. This subsystem links human emissions and natural feedbacks (permafrost) to the temperature response, with ocean and terrestrial sinks providing negative feedback. The stock is initialized to 830 Gt CO2, corresponding to approximately 338 ppm in 1980.

Removal FLOW OUT Ocean Sink + Terrestrial Sink Gt CO₂/yr — Total carbon removal from atmosphere

System 2

Temperature & Albedo

Translates GHG accumulation into surface temperature change through two pathways: direct radiative forcing (GHG effect using the Myhre et al. logarithmic equation) and ice-albedo feedback (Bolch et al.). Surface albedo decreases as glaciers melt and as black carbon deposits on snow, amplifying warming through the R1 and R4 reinforcing loops. Delta Temperature is tracked separately from absolute Temperature to avoid confusion in threshold-based equations (permafrost, snow probability).

Temperature AUX Base Temperature + Delta Temperature °C 14°C at t=0 [17] World Bank Nepal Climate Profile (2021)
Delta Temperature (ΔT) AUX GHG Temp Effect + Albedo Temp Effect °C 0 at t=0 Total warming above 1980 baseline
Base Temperature PARAM 14 °C — [17] Nepal mean surface temperature; World Bank (2021)
GHG Temperature Effect AUX 3.0 × LN(GHG / GHG₀ + 1) / LN(2) °C 0 at t=0 [1,2] Myhre et al. (1998); climate sensitivity 3°C/CO₂ doubling (IPCC AR6)
Albedo Temperature Effect AUX 1.5 × (0.62 − Surface Albedo) °C 0 at t=0 [9] Bolch et al. (2012); reference albedo 0.62 (clean glacier snow)
Surface Albedo AUX MAX(0.15, 0.25 + 0.37 × Ice Fraction − BC Albedo Effect) dmnl 0.62 at t=0 [8] Brun et al. (2017); bare soil 0.25, clean glacier adds 0.37; min 0.15
BC Albedo Effect AUX 0.038 × MIN(1, Black Carbon / Initial BC) dmnl 0.038 at t=0 [12] ICIMOD/ScienceDirect (2021); max 3.8% albedo reduction at full BC load
Ice Fraction AUX MIN(1, MAX(0, Total Ice / Initial Ice)) dmnl 1.0 at t=0 Derived from stocks; 0 = no ice, 1 = full 1980 cover
Total Ice AUX Glaciers Volume + Snow Cover km³ 362 at t=0 [5] Permanent glacier + seasonal snowpack stocks
Initial Ice PARAM 362 km³ — [5] ICIMOD HI-WISE (2023): 312 km³ glacier + 50 km³ snow
System 3

Glaciers & Snow Mass Balance

The core glacier dynamics. Glaciers Volume is fed by compacted snowfall (Glacial Deposit) and depleted by temperature-driven melt, enhanced by black carbon. Snow Cover acts as a buffer stock that converts to glacier ice over time. The melt rate is calibrated to Nepal-specific field data from Maurer et al. (2021) and Stigter et al. (2021), and uses a sigmoid snow probability function following Sherpa et al. (2020). Glacier Area represents the effective high-elevation melting zone (top 10% by elevation) where mass loss dominates.

VariableTypeEquationUnitInitial ValueSource
Glaciers Volume STOCK INTEG(Glacial Deposit − Glacier Melt Rate, 312) km³ 312 [5] ICIMOD HI-WISE (2023): 3,808 Nepal glaciers, total volume 312 km³
Snow Cover STOCK INTEG(Snowfall Precipitation − Glacial Deposit, 50) km³ 50 [8] Brun et al. (2017): HMA snow water equivalent estimates
Glacial Deposit FLOW MAX(0, Accum Rate × Annual Snowfall − Surface Melt Rate × ΔT) km³/yr — [7] Stigter et al. (2021): 55% accumulation fraction (Yala/Rikha Samba)
Snowfall Precipitation FLOW Snow Probability × Base Precipitation × Snow Area km³/yr — [11] Sherpa et al. (2020): catchment area 800 km² at 1.5 km/yr precip
Annual Snowfall AUX Snow Probability × Base Precipitation × Glacier Area km³/yr — Snowfall over effective glacier area used in Glacial Deposit equation
Glacier Melt Rate AUX MAX(0, (Melt Sensitivity + BC Melt Enhancement) × ΔT × Glacier Area) km³/yr 0 at t=0 [6] Maurer et al. (2021): −4.29 km³/yr HMA; calibrated to Nepal 312 km³

Glacier Area PARAM 0.363 km² — [5] ICIMOD (2023): 3,902 km² total; 363 km² = top 10% effective melt zone (units normalized)
Base Precipitation PARAM 1.5 km/yr — [11] Sherpa et al. (2020): Khumbu Valley ~1,200–2,000 mm/yr
Accumulation Rate PARAM 0.55 dmnl — [7] Stigter et al. (2021): 55% of snowfall becomes glacier mass
Surface Melt Rate PARAM 0.25 km³/yr/°C — Calibrated surface melt term in Glacial Deposit equation
System 4

Glacial Lake & GLOF Risk

Meltwater that does not flow directly to rivers collects in proglacial lakes. Warm lake water undercuts glacier termini (thermal undercutting), causing additional ice calving — a self-reinforcing R2 loop. GLOF Risk Index tracks the aggregate threat level, normalized against Nepal's critical aggregate lake volume threshold derived from the 47 priority glacial lakes identified by Rounce et al. PNAS (2020). The Hindu (2024) reports 10.81% area expansion of these lakes between 2011 and 2024, validating the upward trajectory.

VariableTypeEquationUnitInitial ValueSource
Glacial Lake STOCK INTEG(Meltwater Inflow − Lake Drainage, 10) km³ 10 [10,15] Rounce et al. PNAS (2020); The Hindu (2024): 10.81% expansion 2011–2024
Meltwater Inflow FLOW IN Lake Feed Fraction × Glacier Melt Rate + Thermal Undercutting km³/yr — [10] Rounce (2020): 30% of meltwater enters proglacial lakes
Lake Drainage FLOW OUT Drainage Fraction × Glacial Lake km³/yr — [10] Natural seepage through moraine; calibrated 8%/yr drainage rate
Thermal Undercutting AUX Undercutting Coeff × Glacial Lake × MAX(0, ΔT) km³/yr 0 at t=0 Calibrated: at ΔT=2°C, Lake=10 km³ → 0.5 km³/yr extra inflow; drives R2 loop
GLOF Risk Index AUX MIN(1, Glacial Lake / GLOF Threshold) dmnl 0.2 at t=0 [10] 0=safe, 1=critical; normalized against 47-lake aggregate threshold
Lake Feed Fraction PARAM 0.3 dmnl — [10] Rounce et al. (2020): 30% of glacier melt enters proglacial lakes
Drainage Fraction PARAM 0.08 1/yr — [10] PNAS (2020); calibrated to Nepal lake residence times
Undercutting Coefficient PARAM 0.025 1/(yr·°C) — Calibrated; at ΔT=2°C, Lake=10 km³ → 0.5 km³/yr realistic for Nepal
GLOF Threshold PARAM 50 km³ — [10] Nepal 47 priority lakes aggregate critical volume; Rounce et al. PNAS (2020)
System 5

Forest Cover

Nepal's forests act as a terrestrial carbon sink, moderately offsetting GHG emissions. Forest loss is driven by a baseline deforestation rate plus additional pressure from GLOF events (floods damage forest margins). Nepal's community forestry programs since the 1980s have been highly effective, resulting in net afforestation at the national scale. The forest contribution to global GHG removal is small (~0.0025 Gt CO2/yr versus 3.1 Gt/yr global terrestrial baseline), but the B3 mechanism through GLOF creates an indirect structural climate feedback that weakens the B2 balancing loop.

Afforestation FLOW IN Base Afforestation Rate × Forest Cover km²/yr 896 [14] GFW (2024): ~1.4%/yr from community forestry programs
Deforestation FLOW OUT (Base Deforestation Rate + Flood Pressure) × Forest Cover km²/yr 768 [14] GFW (2024): ~1.2%/yr baseline loss; GLOF events add extra pressure
Flood Pressure AUX 0.002 × GLOF Risk Index 1/yr 0.0002 at t=0 [5] Assumption: max 0.2% extra deforestation rate at full GLOF risk

Base Deforestation Rate PARAM 0.012 1/yr — [14] GFW (2024): historical loss rate before community forestry recovery
System 6

Black Carbon

Black carbon (BC) from South Asian combustion sources deposits on Himalayan glaciers, reducing albedo and directly enhancing melt. The BC stock grows with regional development and has an atmospheric lifetime of approximately 2 years before washout. ICIMOD (2021) attributes 39% of pre-monsoon Himalayan glacier melt to BC deposition, and 69% of BC sources to India and Nepal combined. This creates the R4 reinforcing loop where warming increases melt that exposes more dark ice, further reducing surface albedo.

4 Testing Policy Interventions

To test policy scenarios, modify the parameters listed below in Vensim using the Sensitivity or SyntheSim tools. Compare Glaciers Volume, GLOF Risk Index, and Delta Temperature trajectories across runs. All scenarios start from the same 1980 initial conditions.


Scenario What to Change Parameter Values Expected Effect
Business as Usual (BAU) No changes Default values Glaciers lose ~40% by 2080; GLOF risk peaks ~2060; ΔT approaches 2.5–3°C
Paris 1.5°C Target Reduce Human Emission growth rate Growth rate: 0.018 → −0.02/yr (emissions peak ~2030, then decline) ΔT stays under 1.5°C; permafrost loop (R3) remains inactive; glacier loss <20% by 2080

5 Model Limitations

  • The forest-GHG link is negligible at Nepal scale. Nepal sequesters only ~0.0025 Gt CO₂/yr versus the global terrestrial baseline of 3.1 Gt/yr (less than 0.1% contribution). The B2/B3 loops have meaningful structural presence but minimal quantitative impact on global GHG trajectories.
  • The GHG subsystem is global, not Nepal-local. Temperature response is a simplified global mean sensitivity (3°C per CO₂ doubling), whereas actual Nepal Himalayan warming has been approximately 1.8× the global mean. This likely underestimates regional warming in the Himalayan zone.
  • No spatial heterogeneity between glaciers. The model uses aggregate volumes (312 km³, 3,808 glaciers) and cannot represent differences between large valley glaciers and small cirque glaciers, or east-west precipitation gradients across Nepal.
  • No monsoon variability. Precipitation is modeled as a constant annual rate with a sigmoid snow probability function. Interannual variability in monsoon intensity — a major driver of glacier mass balance — is not captured in the current formulation.
  • GLOF dynamics use aggregate lake volume, not individual lake physics. Real GLOF events are discrete, threshold-triggered dam-breach events. The continuous flow formulation smooths out this fundamentally discontinuous behavior.
  • Black Carbon uses a South Asia regional stock, not Nepal-specific deposition rates. Local deposition efficiency varies with wind patterns and altitude, and the model does not distinguish between BC deposited on clean snow versus exposed glacier ice.
